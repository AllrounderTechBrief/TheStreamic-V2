Two Years Later: How the Paris Games Redefined Remote Cloud Production for Global Events

What was deployed as temporary infrastructure for Paris 2024 is now the permanent workflow for global live events. The cloud production experiment worked — and the industry has quietly absorbed the lessons.

By The Streamic Editorial Team March 19, 2026 1240 words · 6 min read

In the months before Paris 2024, several major rights holders made a decision that would have been considered reckless two Olympic cycles earlier: they would produce significant portions of their Games coverage without a traditional broadcast centre anywhere near France.

The workflows that enabled this — cloud-based production, remote commentary contribution, IP-native graphics and replay, and centralised distribution from facilities thousands of kilometres from the venues — had been maturing for years. Paris was the proof of concept at the largest scale the industry had ever attempted.

Two years on, the experiment is no longer an experiment. The workflows that were stress-tested in Paris are now the standard production architecture for global events at the major rights holders. The "temporary" infrastructure was permanent the moment it worked.

What the Paris Deployments Actually Looked Like

The Paris cloud production story was not a single deployment — it was dozens of parallel experiments by different rights holders, each making different bets on which elements of the production chain could move to cloud and which needed to remain on-site.

The common thread was a centralised production hub in each rights holder's home territory, receiving compressed but high-quality contribution feeds from venue-based cameras and microphones. Commentary, replay, graphics, and final programme assembly all happened remotely, with the venue presence reduced to camera operators, audio capture, and the contribution encoding point.

The latency management challenge that had limited remote production in earlier attempts was addressed through a combination of JPEG XS contribution — which provides near-lossless quality at manageable bitrates — and cloud-based production tools from Vizrt, Ross Video, and Grass Valley that were rebuilt to function within the latency constraints of IP contribution rather than SDI baseband.

The Numbers Behind the Shift

The operational case for centralised cloud production at major events rests on a set of numbers that have become clearer since Paris:

Venue footprint reduction of 40–60 percent compared to traditional fly-away production was consistently reported by facilities that completed the transition. This translates directly into reduced logistics, accommodation, and venue services costs — typically the largest line items in a major event production budget.

Carbon footprint reduction was a secondary but increasingly important metric. Fewer crew travelling to venues, less equipment shipped, and the elimination of diesel generator sets for temporary power were each meaningful reductions. Several rights holders published detailed carbon accounting post-Games that showed total production footprint reductions of 25–35 percent versus their previous cycle equivalent.

Workflow continuity across time zones proved to be an unexpected operational gain. Centralised production hubs could hand off between teams across working hours more smoothly than distributed venue crews, reducing the fatigue-related quality issues that are endemic in extended live event coverage.

What Failed, and What Was Fixed

The Paris deployments were not uniformly successful, and the post-event engineering reviews are informative about where the technology was not yet mature enough for the demands placed on it.

Internet contribution paths — used by rights holders who could not justify the cost of dedicated fibre circuits for smaller venue pools — proved unreliable under the network congestion that accompanies major global events. The fallback to higher-latency encoding paths affected several productions during peak viewing periods. The lesson absorbed has been unambiguous: contribution infrastructure for Tier 1 events needs dedicated circuits.

Remote replay systems performed well on pre-configured sequences but required higher-than-expected operator density when covering sports with complex multi-angle requirements. The interfaces for cloud-based replay have been updated since Paris, but this remains an area where the gap between SDI-native and IP-native operational experience is still visible.

The 2026 Standard

For major global events in 2026, the Paris architecture is now the baseline. Rights holders who were observers in 2024 have since deployed pilot versions of centralised cloud production for regional events — the standard pathway for building organisational confidence before committing to this model at an Olympic or World Cup scale.

The technology has continued to mature. JPEG XS is now supported natively in a wider range of cameras and encoders, reducing the contribution chain complexity that characterised early deployments. Cloud production tools have improved their latency compensation, and the operational documentation for remote production workflows has become detailed enough that facilities without prior experience can plan deployments with reasonable accuracy.

What Paris demonstrated that no amount of laboratory testing could have proved is that centralised cloud production at the highest level of live sports and events is operationally viable. The industry now knows this from direct experience rather than vendor assurance. The next challenge is making the economics work at the tier below the major rights holders — regional sports, premium news events, and cultural programming — where the capital efficiency arguments are even more compelling but the engineering resource to execute the transition is thinner.

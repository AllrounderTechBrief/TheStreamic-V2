The ST 2110 Transition: Why Small-Market Broadcasters Are Choosing 'Hybrid-IP' Over Full Migration

Full IP migration is technically achievable. But for hundreds of regional broadcasters, the cost and complexity of a complete SDI-to-IP move makes hybrid gateways the only rational path. This is the infrastructure reality that the trade press rarely covers.

By The Streamic Editorial Team March 20, 2026 1320 words · 7 min read

The SMPTE ST 2110 standard has been commercially available since 2017. Nine years later, the majority of the world's broadcast facilities still route video over coaxial cable. Understanding why is not a story about technical failure — it is a story about economic reality.

For the major networks, national broadcasters, and premium sports rights holders, full IP migration has been the right call. The operational gains in flexibility, remote production capability, and future-proofing justify the capital investment and the years of engineering effort required to complete it.

For the hundreds of regional and small-market broadcasters that together account for the majority of broadcast facilities globally, the calculus is entirely different. And the answer most of them have arrived at is hybrid-IP — a pragmatic middle path that is receiving far less attention than it deserves.

The Cost Problem

A full ST 2110 deployment requires an IP network capable of transporting uncompressed video — which means 10GbE or 25GbE infrastructure throughout the facility, PTP-synchronised timing, and IP-native equipment at every production and playout point.

For a regional broadcaster operating with a 2010-vintage SDI infrastructure, the bill for a complete rip-and-replace migration starts at several million pounds and rises significantly with facility complexity. The equipment cost is substantial. The engineering project cost — planning, testing, commissioning, staff training, production downtime — often exceeds it.

Against this, the operational benefit for a facility running 4–6 live channels from a single site is real but limited. The remote production gains that justify IP migration for a major sports broadcaster are less compelling when your most complex remote production is a council chamber.

What Gateway Technology Actually Provides

The hybrid-IP approach centres on SDI/IP gateway devices — appliances that bridge existing SDI infrastructure with IP-native equipment. Leading products from Riedel, Nevion, and Grass Valley allow facilities to introduce IP-capable equipment at specific points in the workflow without replacing the underlying SDI fabric.

The practical workflow typically looks like this: SDI cameras and traditional routing infrastructure remain in place. A gateway converts the SDI output to ST 2110 at the point where new IP-native processing is needed — a software-defined vision mixer, an IP-based graphics system, or a cloud production interface. The output returns to SDI before hitting legacy distribution or transmission equipment.

This approach allows facilities to:

  • Introduce IP-native production technology without replacing functional SDI infrastructure
  • Build operational competency with IP workflows on a limited scope before committing to full migration
  • Defer capital expenditure on network infrastructure until existing SDI equipment reaches end of life
  • Maintain existing transmission and distribution paths that are still under support contracts

The PTP Timing Challenge

One aspect of hybrid deployments that is consistently underestimated is the timing infrastructure. ST 2110 requires Precision Time Protocol (PTP) synchronisation, which has different characteristics to the traditional sync generators (SPGs) that most facilities operate.

In a hybrid environment, both systems coexist. The engineering challenge is ensuring that the PTP-synchronised IP section and the SPG-synchronised SDI section remain locked at the gateway without introducing timing anomalies visible in the output.

Facilities that have navigated this successfully typically describe it as the hardest part of the hybrid deployment — not because the technology is fundamentally difficult, but because the interaction between the two timing systems creates edge cases that only emerge under specific operational conditions and require detailed understanding of both domains to diagnose.

The Skills Gap Is Real

Broadcast IP engineering is a different discipline from traditional broadcast engineering, and the skills gap is one of the most significant constraints on migration timelines for smaller facilities.

A senior SDI engineer can troubleshoot a timing or routing problem with a waveform monitor and decades of pattern recognition. An IP network problem requires different tools — packet capture analysis, network topology understanding, PTP debugging — and a different mental model of how signals flow through a facility.

The hybrid approach has a practical advantage here: it limits the scope of IP engineering required. A gateway deployment means that IP competency needs to extend only to the boundaries of the IP island, rather than throughout the facility. This makes the skills investment proportionate to the operational benefit being captured.

The Vendor Support Landscape

The economics of the gateway market have improved significantly. Where gateway devices were premium products three years ago, competitive pressure has driven prices down, and the major vendors now offer gateway functionality as part of broader infrastructure products rather than standalone appliances.

Support for hybrid configurations has also improved. Vendors whose business model depends on selling IP-native equipment to facilities that still operate primarily in SDI have a commercial incentive to make the gateway experience as smooth as possible — which has been reflected in better documentation, more realistic migration guides, and improved interoperability testing.

The Five-Year View

For small-market broadcasters, the hybrid-IP approach is not a final destination — it is a bridge. The realistic planning horizon for most facilities is a full IP migration aligned with the next major infrastructure refresh cycle, typically 7–10 years out.

What the hybrid phase provides is the operational experience, staff competency, and organisational confidence to make that migration lower-risk when it does occur. Facilities that have operated IP islands for several years consistently report that their full migration, when it comes, is faster and smoother than they had planned.

The ST 2110 story is not a story of adoption failure. It is a story of a technology that is being absorbed at a pace that reflects the economic and operational reality of the facilities deploying it — which is exactly how durable broadcast technology transitions have always worked.
